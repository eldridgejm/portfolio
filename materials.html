Lecture Slides

Highlights

In total, I have created over 6,800 slides for classes I have taught in the last 5 years. To make it easier to get a sense of my lecture style and the effort I put into the slides, I have below highlighted three lectures which I feel are exemplary.

Lecture on k-d Trees, DSC 190 - Advanced Algorithms

Slides

Videos: part 1, part 2, part 3, part 4, part 5, part 6

This lecture on k-d trees is written for an upper-division elective on advanced algorithms and data structures for data scientists. This particular lecture was delivered remotely via pre-recorded YouTube videos.

These slides demonstrate a structure I commonly use in my lectures:

  • I start by motivating the problem of finding nearest neighbors by stating its importance in a data science context (e.g., the nearest neighbor classifier).
  • I briefly explore the "naive" solution to the problem (brute force search) and its detriments.
  • I improve upon the "naive" solution with an intuitive solution (build a grid) that students likely have thought of. I identify what needs to be done to make it a good solution.
  • Having identified the problems with "build a grid" idea, I outline the fix (k-d trees).
  • Once the algorithm or data structure is introduced, I go through several reinforcing examples.
  • I end with a discussion of the limitations of the method we just learned, often with a teaser for the next lecture's topic.

Lecture on Asymptotic Notation, DSC 40B

Slides

This lecture on asymptotic notation and time complexity analysis is written for a lower-division course on computer science foundations for data scientists.

These slides highlight several techniques I've come to use often:

  • Outlining thought processes: When introducing students to a new problem-solving technique, I often outline my own thought process in a series of steps. In this lecture, this is demonstrated by the strategy provided for working with chains of inequalities, as well as the strategy for proving or disproving statements about asymptotic notation.
  • Excercises: My lectures typically contain several "excercise" slides. At these points in the lecture, I pause for a couple of minutes to let students discuss the exercise amongst themselves. Oftentimes, the exercise is directly relevant to a problem that appears on an assignment, so students are well-motivated to tackle them during class.
  • Organization: definitions, main ideas, and exercises are all clearly marked using different colors so that students can easily come back to find them when reviewing the slides. When I identify common mistakes made by students, I add a big red "Warning" to the slides to allow future students to learn from the mistakes of the others. Lectures are broken into several small parts. In-person lectures are recorded at UCSD, and I will often re-upload the lecture to YouTube and link directly to each lecture part on the course page, enabling students to jump right to a topic that they wish to review.

In their end-of-quarter evaluations, students frequently highlight how the design and structure of the lecture slides greatly assist in their learning. For example:

Professor Eldridge is an amazing professor. He has amazing succinct presentations which are very neat and orderly. He explains concepts nice and slowly, making sure you don't miss a beat. I appreciate his strong design sense and color coding. He also has great handwriting. All of these small things add up to form a great course, where all content is modular and easy to digest [...]

Lecture on the Spectral Theorem, DSC 140B

Slides

This lecture on the spectral theorem and eigenvectors is written for an upper-division machine learning course. While students have taken linear algebra and have seen the concepts in this lecture before, they are usually unsure of why eigenvectors are useful quantities and what they mean, intuitively. This lecture comes after a series of lectures which remind students that matrices are concise "encodings" of linear transformations.

These slides are meant to demonstrate how I sometimes take novel approaches to building intuition behind complex ideas. In this lecture, I repeatedly visualize linear transformations (matrices) by plotting their action on a set of unit vectors (such visualizations were explained in earlier lectures). We see that when the matrix is symmetric, the picture is symmetric, too, and that its axes of symmetry are described by the eigenvectors. This gives students a visual intuition for what eigenvectors are.

We finish by realizing that the eigenvectors of the transformation are maximizers/minimizers; and are therefore useful in solving some of the optimization problems we often encounter in data science. Later lectures on PCA and Laplacian Eigenmaps reinforce this.

Software Packages and Code

  • dsc40graph
    A pedagogical Python package providing a sample implementation of a graph data structure, used by students to complete assignments in DSC 40B. The code is freely available on GitHub for students to learn from, and it is freely licensed for others to use.
  • dsctex
    A collection of LaTeX classes and style files used by myself and other instructors to create course materials (slides, assignments, exams, etc.), and by students to complete assignments.
  • babypandas

    A pedagogical tabular data analysis library used in DSC 10, babypandas was created by Aaron Fraenkel; I have contributed to it, and maintained it in the time since Aaron left UCSD. babypandas is a subset of the popular pandas Python package used throughout industry that is designed to be easier to learn. While there are potentially many ways to do the same thing in pandas, there is typically only one way to do the same thing in babypandas.

    In addition to maintaining and contributing to the codebase, I (along with an undergraduate student, Parker Addison) wrote the Notes on (Baby)Pandas, a set of course notes for DSC 10 introducing students to the package.

  • gradelib
    A Python package for computing overall grades. It implements some of the grading policies that I use in my classes, such as redemption and slip days.
  • autogradescope An autograder library that provides more helpful messages to students. Used in DSC 40B and DSC 190 - Representation Learning.

Demos

The below is a sampling of interactive visualizations and Jupyter notebooks I have created. Visualizations are often used in lecture to support the explanation of complex concepts. On the other hand, Jupyter notebook demos are often provided as supplementary materials for students who wish to do a "deeper dive" into a particular topic. They often contain practical tips on implementing and using a method.

  • Interactive Visualization: Maximum Likelihood Estimation
    This interactive visualization (best viewed in Google Chrome) is used during a DSC 140A lecture to explain the method of Maximum Likelihood in parametric density estimation.
  • Interactive Visualization: PCA
    This interactive visualization (best viewed in Google Chrome) is used during a DSC 140B lecture on PCA to clarify the notion of "direction of maximum variance", and to demonstrate how the direction of maximum variance minimizes the reconstruction error.
  • Jupyter Notebook: Practical Performance of Tries in Python
    In this demo Jupyter notebook, students implement a trie data structure in Python and measure its efficiency, finding that it is actually worse than a naive algorithm. This demonstrates the cost of implementing algorithms and data structures in a high-level language like Python.
  • Jupyter Notebook: Locality Sensitive Hashing
    In this Jupyter notebook demo, students implement Locality Sensitive Hashing and find that it works surprisingly well on image retrieval.
  • Jupyter Notebook: PCA in Practice
    A Jupyter notebook demonstrating how to use PCA on image data. Students can run the demo, change the code, and see what effect it has.
  • Jupyter Notebook: Perceptron Training
    In this Jupyter notebook, students explore the iterative process used to train a perceptron. It includes an animation of the decision boundary and it's progress over the course of the algorithm, as well as a visualization of the perceptron's parameters. Students can look into the "brain" of the perceptron and see it learn to distinguish between handwritten digits in real time.

Assignments

The following is a sampling of assignments I have created.

  • DSC 10 Project - Chicago Taxi Rides
    In this DSC 10 project, students are guided through a data science project exploring a data dump of taxis rides in Chicago. They see the potential of relatively simple data analysis techniques when irregularities in the data are followed and found to suggest fraduluent fees added by potentially dishonest drivers.
  • DSC 80 Project - COVID Vaccinations
    In this DSC 80 project, students explore a COVID vaccination data set and see how the overall effectiveness of the vaccine can be misleading; the effectiveness within each individual age group is greater than the overall effectiveness. This demonstrates the unintuitive Simpson's paradox.
  • DSC 40B - Super Homework
    In this last homework of the quarter, students "tie everything together" in Programming Problem 01, which asks them to write efficient algorithms to solve an optimization problem that arises in training a classifier in one dimension. Students not only need to draw upon concepts from throughout the quarter -- including hashing and utilizing sorted structure to design faster algorithms -- but also see how algorithm design and data structure choice both play a role in machine learning.
  • DSC 140A - Homework 04

    This homework demonstrates two techniques I often use in creating assignments. The first problem demonstrates a form of scaffolding, where I guide students through a problem-solving process. If the problem were given without breaking it into parts, students might feel lost; instead, I try to make each part challenging, but manageable. To complete part (a), students will likely need to remember that we did a very similar procedure in an earlier lecture to derive the normal equations for least squares regression. In part (b), they will draw upon a lecture where we derived a vector calculus identity. And in part (c), they will need to recognize that optimizing the risk can be done by finding the gradient, and that their answers to parts (a) and (b) are useful in that regard.

    The second problem demonstrates another technique I like to use, where I have students "rediscover" a key idea by proving it empirically. In lecture, I tried to convey the brilliance of the kernel trick by stating that kernel ridge regression is equivalent to ridge regression in feature space, but it can be hard to grasp what this means in the heat of lecture. So in this problem I have students prove it to themselves by training both models and showing that they are the same. The problem is designed so as to allow students to check their own work, since the predictions made by both models should agree if their work is correct. This gives them instant feedback.

  • DSC 40B - Lab 02

    This is is lab assignment for DSC 40B. Labs consist of a small number (usually 10) questions that serve as a simple check on understanding. The labs are autograded, but if a student misses a question, they can regain (most) of the lost credit by talking to a teaching assistant and describing their misconception; I refer to this policy as "lab redemption". This mechanism causes students to not only review their mistakes, but to engage with course staff. Exam questions are modeled after lab questions (and lab questions are typically former exam questions), and so students are highly motivated to understand the labs and know what to expect on the tests.

    A student had this to say about the course's redemption policy.

    This was an amazing course that allowed for so much growth and learning. I was constantly reviewing my previous homework and learning from my mistakes allowing me to keep updated on the material. Being able to submit regrades encouraged me to learn from my mistakes as well as the discussions were incredibly useful for the homeworks. I found there aren't enough office hours, but it was still doable. I learned a great deal and the professor made the material seem so much easier to process.

Exams

  • DSC 40B Midterm

    This midterm from DSC 40B shows how exam questions are similar to lab questions (see the example DSC 40B lab above). It also shows how I create complete solutions, often with explanations, which are distributed to students afterwards. I also create recap videos going over the exam's solutions and my thought process for solving each problem. Exams are eligible for "redemption", meaning that students can replace their midterm score by taking a "redemption exam" covering the same content at the end of the quarter.
