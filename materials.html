Lecture Slides

Highlights

In total, I have created over 6,800 slides for classes I have taught in the last 5 years. To make it easier to get a sense of my lecture style and the effort I put into the slides, I have below highlighted three lectures which I feel are exemplary.

Lecture on k-d Trees, DSC 190 - Advanced Algorithms

Slides

This lecture on k-d trees is written for an upper-division elective on advanced algorithms and data structures for data scientists. This particular lecture was delivered remotely via pre-recorded YouTube videos.

These slides demonstrate a structure I commonly use in my lectures:

  • I start by motivating the problem of finding nearest neighbors by stating its importance in a data science context (e.g., the nearest neighbor classifier).
  • I briefly explore the "naive" solution to the problem (brute force search) and its detriments.
  • I improve upon the "naive" solution with an intuitive solution (build a grid) that students likely have thought of. I identify what needs to be done to make it a good solution.
  • Having identified the problems with "build a grid" idea, I outline the fix (k-d trees).
  • Once the algorithm or data structure is introduced, I go through several reinforcing examples.
  • I end with a discussion of the limitations of the method we just learned, often with a teaser for the next lecture's topic.

Lecture on Asymptotic Notation, DSC 40B

Slides

This lecture on asymptotic notation and time complexity analysis is written for a lower-division course on computer science foundations for data scientists.

These slides highlight several techniques I've come to use often:

  • Outlining thought processes: When introducing students to a new problem-solving technique, I often outline my own thought process in a series of steps. In this lecture, this is demonstrated by the strategy provided for working with chains of inequalities, as well as the strategy for proving or disproving statements about asymptotic notation.
  • Excercises: My lectures typically contain several "excercise" slides. At these points in the lecture, I pause for a couple of minutes to let students discuss the exercise amongst themselves. Oftentimes, the exercise is directly relevant to a problem that appears on an assignment, so students are well-motivated to tackle them during class.
  • Organization: definitions, main ideas, and exercises are all clearly marked using different colors so that students can easily come back to find them when reviewing the slides. When I identify common mistakes made by students, I add a big red "Warning" to the slides to allow future students to learn from the mistakes of the others. Lectures are broken into several small parts. In-person lectures are recorded at UCSD, and I will often re-upload the lecture to YouTube and link directly to each lecture part on the course page, enabling students to jump right to a topic that they wish to review.

In their end-of-quarter evaluations, students frequently highlight how the design and structure of the lecture slides greatly assist in their learning. For example:

Professor Eldridge is an amazing professor. He has amazing succinct presentations which are very neat and orderly. He explains concepts nice and slowly, making sure you don't miss a beat. I appreciate his strong design sense and color coding. He also has great handwriting. All of these small things add up to form a great course, where all content is modular and easy to digest [...]

Lecture on the Spectral Theorem, DSC 140B

Slides

This lecture on the spectral theorem and eigenvectors is written for an upper-division machine learning course. While students have taken linear algebra and have seen the concepts in this lecture before, they are usually unsure of why eigenvectors are useful quantities and what they mean, intuitively. This lecture comes after a series of lectures which remind students that matrices are concise "encodings" of linear transformations.

These slides are meant to demonstrate how I sometimes take novel approaches to building intuition behind complex ideas. In this lecture, I repeatedly visualize linear transformations (matrices) by plotting their action on a set of unit vectors (such visualizations were explained in earlier lectures). We see that when the matrix is symmetric, the picture is symmetric, too, and that its axes of symmetry are described by the eigenvectors. This gives students a visual intuition for what eigenvectors are.

We finish by realizing that the eigenvectors of the transformation are maximizers/minimizers; and are therefore useful in solving some of the optimization problems we often encounter in data science. Later lectures on PCA and Laplacian Eigenmaps reinforce this.

Software Packages and Code

  • dsc40graph
    A pedagogical Python package providing a sample implementation of a graph data structure, used by students to complete assignments in DSC 40B. The code is freely available on GitHub for students to learn from, and it is freely licensed for others to use.
  • dsctex
    A collection of LaTeX classes and style files used by myself and other instructors to create course materials (slides, assignments, exams, etc.), and by students to complete assignments.
  • babypandas

    A pedagogical tabular data analysis library used in DSC 10, babypandas was created by Aaron Fraenkel; I have contributed to it, and maintained it in the time since Aaron left UCSD. babypandas is a subset of the popular pandas Python package used throughout industry that is designed to be easier to learn. While there are potentially many ways to do the same thing in pandas, there is typically only one way to do the same thing in babypandas.

    In addition to maintaining and contributing to the codebase, I (along with an undergraduate student, Parker Addison) wrote the Notes on (Baby)Pandas, a set of course notes for DSC 10 introducing students to the package.

  • gradelib
    A Python package for computing overall grades. It implements some of the grading policies that I use
