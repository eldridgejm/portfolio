  homeworks and "check-offs"

Your grade in this class will come from two types of assessments: homeworks and exams. But the homeworks and exams in this quarter's version of CSE 151A might be a little different than what you are used to. That's because a large number of students will be taking this class P/NP. The grading scheme and assessments are designed to reflect that.

Homework problems will be a mixture of theory and practice. There will be problems that require you to write code, but you'll be able to use whatever language you prefer.

"essential" and "plus" problems

Each homework problem falls into one of two possible categories: it is either an "essential" problem, or it is a "plus" problem. Essential problems are mandatory; everyone, regardless of whether they are taking the class P/NP or for a letter grade, must attempt every essential problem. But the plus problems are optional. It is by attempting the plus problems that you can get a high letter grade in the course; see grading below for how.

The essential problems and plus problems differ in how they are graded. Essential problems are graded on a color scale:

  • Green: You had the right idea, and you get credit for the problem.
  • Yellow: Your approach showed effort, but wasn't right. You'll need to meet with a tutor or TA to get your answer "checked-off", at which point it will be regraded to Green. See check-offs below for more on the process.
  • Red: You didn't answer the problem, or showed no work. No credit is awarded and you are not eligible for a check-off.

At the end of the quarter, almost all of your solutions to essential problems must be marked Green (either by getting the problem correct or by checking it off with a tutor) in order to pass the class. See grading below for specifics.

"Plus" problems, on the other hand, are graded with partial credit on a traditional point scale, with some problems being worth more than others. In order to receive an A, you'll need to earn above a certain number of points on plus problems throughout the quarter. See grading below for specifics.

"check-offs"

Suppose you have incorrectly answered an "essential" problem, and it was marked Yellow. You must get your solution checked-off by a tutor within one week of your assignment being returned.

To get a Yellow solution checked-off:

  1. Review your answer to find your mistake, comparing it to the solutions posted on the course webpage.
  2. Submit a regrade request for the problem on Gradescope. In your regrade request, identify your mistake and what you would need to do to correct it. You don't need to go into a ton of detail here; three or four sentences should do it.
  3. A grader will review your request within a week (as long as you submit it within a week of the homework scores being posted, your regrade request will be reviewed). If you aren't able to identify what you did incorrectly, you'll be asked to attend a grader's office hours in order to discuss the problem in more detail.

Check-offs aren't punishments; they're designed to get quick, targeted feedback to those who need it.

Warning! If your Yellow solution isn't checked-off within a week of receiving your grade, it will automatically lapse to Red.

slip days

You have four "slip days" to use throughout the quarter. A slip day extends the deadline of any one homework by 24 hours. Slip days can't be "stacked" — the latest any one homework can be submitted is 24 hours after the deadline. Slip days are applied automatically at the end of the quarter, but it's your responsibility to keep track of how many you have left.

frequency and due dates

There will be eight homeworks. Each homework will be due via Gradescope at 11:59 PM on the Wednesday after it is assigned.

collaboration

You may discuss the homework problems with other people, but you must turn in your original work. Sharing "big-picture" ideas about how to solve problems is fine; sharing the details or complete solutions is not. If you submit a solution that looks substantially the same as another person's, you're putting yourself at risk of an Academic Integrity report.

Here's a rule of thumb to help you decide if your mode of collaboration is OK: wait an hour after discussing a problem to write down your answer. If you're having trouble, that means you might not fully understand the solution.

regrade requests

If you feel that the grader has made a mistake, you may submit a regrade request via Gradescope within one week of the grades being released. Note that part of your grade is clarity, so if your answer was mostly right but unclear you may still not deserve full credit.

To get an "essential" problem regraded, don't use the normal regrade request mechanism on Gradescope. Instead, follow the procedure to get your solution "checked-off"

  exams

There will be two midterms and one final exam, held on the following dates:

  • Midterm 01: Friday, May 1 (covers weeks 01 — 04)
  • Midterm 02: Friday, May 29 (covers weeks 05 — 08)
  • Final Exam: Wednesday, June 10

The exams will be online and delivered via Canvas quiz or similar. There will be a window of 24 hours during which you can start your exam, but once your exam is started you will have a limited amount of time to finish it: 1.5 hours for each midterm and 4 hours for the final exam ( Update Due to a university rule, online exams cannot exceed 3 hours. As a result, I'm going to reduce the amount of content on the final exam redemption sections and allow a total of 1.5 hours for each). The window will open at 00:00 Pacific Standard Time on the day listed above, and will end at 23:59 Pacific Standard Time.

The exams will be designed to test your knowledge of the "essential" parts of the class. They will consist of 20 to 30 multiple-choice, true/false, or short answer questions that should each take a minute or two to read and solve.

In a "traditional" quarter, exams are the "main event", and the purpose of homework is to prepare you for the exam. In this quarter, the homeworks are the main assessment, and the purpose of the exams is to verify that your performance on the homework is a good signal of your understanding. As such, the best way to prepare for the exams will be to review the "essential" homework problems and make sure that the solutions make sense to you.

Redemption

The final exam will be split into three parts:

  1. The mandatory final exam section, covering weeks 09 and 10. ( Update There will be no mandatory section.)
  2. An optional Midterm 01 Redemption section
  3. An optional Midterm 02 Redemption section

If your score on the midterm redemption section is higher than your score on the original midterm, it will replace that grade. Getting a lower score on a redemption section cannot hurt you (but it will make me sad). As a consequence, the redemption sections are optional.

  grading scheme

There will be no overall "score" in this class that determines your final grade. Instead, the grade that you'll receive will depend on satisfying certain conditions. This is designed so that it is very clear what needs to be done in order to receive a P grade if you are taking the class P/NP.

P/NP

Here is what you'll have to do in order to pass the class, if you are taking the class P/NP:

  • Have at most four essential homework problems with Red grades at the end of the quarter.
  • Receive at least a 70% on both of the midterms (or the redemption sections).
  • Receive at least a 50% on the final exam mandatory section.

You must meet each of the above conditions to pass; otherwise you'll receive a grade of NP.

Remember that Yellow homework scores lapse to Red unless you get them checked-off, in which case they turn to Green.

Letter Grading

Here is how your grade will be calculated if you elect the letter grade option. Each letter grade requires a certain level of performance on both the exams and the homework. Here are the requirements for exams:

Letter Grade Exam Requirements
C-/C/C+ Receive at least a 70% on both of the midterms (or midterm redemption sections), and at least a 50% on the final exam.
B-/B/B+ Receive at least a 80% on both of the midterms (or midterm redemption sections), and at least a 65% on the final exam.
A-/A/A+ Receive at least a 90% on both of the midterms (or midterm redemption sections), and at least a 80% on the final exam.

For homeworks, you must have at most four essential homework problems with Red grades at the end of the quarter to receive a letter grade higher than an F.

You can also earn a higher grade by correctly completing the "plus" problems on each homework. Here is number of plus points you'll need to earn over the course of the quarter in order to receive each letter grade:

Letter Grade # of plus points required
C- ≥ 0
C ≥ 10
C+ ≥ 20
B- ≥ 30
B ≥ 40
B+ ≥ 50
A- ≥ 60
A ≥ 70

I'll guarantee that at least 80 "plus points" will be available throughout the quarter, but I might offer more. The requirements listed above are absolute, meaning that, for instance, earning 50 plus points gets you a B+, whether 80 points are available or 180.

In order to receive a certain letter grade, you must satisfy both the exam and homework requirements for that grade. Another way of putting this is that you'll receive the lower of the exam letter grade and the homework letter grade.

Example

Suppose I get an 85% on both of my midterms and a 90% on the final; this puts me into the "B-/B/B+" category for the exam grade. Then suppose I earn 80 "plus points", putting me into the "A" category for the homework grade. My overall letter grade will be a "B+", since I didn't meet the exam requirements for an "A".

  illnesses and extenuating circumstances

Because of the pandemic, we must prepare for the unfortunate possibility that you will get sick and be unable to participate in this class for long periods of time. The university has a mechanism for helping in this situation: the Incomplete. If you are unable to complete the course because of reasons outside of your control, you may be given an Incomplete instead of a letter grade or a P/NP. This simply means that you will complete the rest of the work at a later time. Once you have done so, your overall grade is calculated and your Incomplete grade is replaced.

An Incomplete does not allow you to re-do work that has already been completed, only to do work that hasn't been completed.

Unfortunately, your personal health is not the only think that might prevent you from participating in this class. Some of us will get sick, others will have family members fall ill, and others might lose their jobs. If you have any doubt about your ability to perform satisfactorily in this course due to something outside of your control, please contact me ASAP and we'll figure something out.

  frequently asked questions

Do I need a webcam for this class?

Nope! If you have one, you're welcome to use it; it can be especially useful to share your work during office hours. But we won't require webcams. In particular, we are not using proctoring services like examity.

Why is it not possible to earn a letter grade of D?

At UCSD, a grade of C- or higher corresponds to a P (pass), and anything lower than a C- corresponds to a NP (not pass). As such, I've made the requirements for a C- the same as the requirements for a P.

Do I need to tell you if I'm taking the class P/NP or for a letter grade?

You don't need to tell me whether you're taking the class P/NP or for a letter grade — you tell the registrar, and I'll submit your grade accordingly at the end of the quarter.

What happens if the professor gets sick and can't run the course?

We've prepared for this possibility in a couple of ways. Namely:

  1. "Backup" faculty have been selected who are ready to take over the course.
  2. All grades will be stored in Canvas so that they are accessible to the university.

Why should I try for a letter grade if I can just take the class P/NP?

First, remember that even though the university has relaxed the constraints on P/NP, that doesn't affect how people outside of the university think of P/NP. If someone looks over your grades in the future, hopefully they'll remember that a global health crisis was occurring in Spring 2020 and that taking the class P/NP shouldn't be interpreted negatively.

But secondly, I've purposely designed the grading scale so that getting a grade of B+, A-, or A is very attainable. For example, to get a B+ score on your homework, you only need to earn 50 plus points when there are at least 80 available.

Since lecture is online, there is no physical limitation on class size. So we can let everybody in off the waitlist, right?

We don't have a classroom size limitation, but we only have so many tutors/TAs (and I don't think we can hire more at this point). That's the real limitation. If we scaled the class up without also scaling the number of TAs, I think we'd get overwhelmed. That said, all of my materials are going to be publicly available on cse151a.com, so if you're unable to make it in off the waitlist you can at least audit the class!
